\begin{abstract}
This paper compares the explanations supported by online planning
and deep reinforcement learning when both agents are asked to
justify the same decision on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}. For each agent, per-action
evidence is extracted at every decision state, counterfactual Monte
Carlo rollouts in the PPO case and the search tree in the MCTS
case, and passed through an identically-formatted LLM explainer
that produces a natural-language rationale, a counterfactual, and a
list of verifiable numerical claims. Explanations are scored by an
independent LLM judge on three metrics: fidelity, soundness, and
post-hoc inferability. Across 289 decision states drawn from two
\emph{performance-matched} agents (both at $1.000$ task-success),
with PPO counterfactual rollouts at $N=100$ stochastic samples per
action, the two evidence sources produce statistically
indistinguishable explanation quality on every metric. A previous
run of the same pipeline at mismatched performance ($0.590$ for PPO
versus $1.000$ for MCTS) had found a small but statistically
supported MCTS advantage on fidelity and soundness; the
performance-matched run shows that gap was a competence confound
rather than a structural property of the
search-tree-vs-learned-policy paradigms. Once the learned policy is
competent, its counterfactual rollouts are as faithful and sound an
evidence source as the deliberation tree.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
When a reinforcement learning agent chooses an action, the action
itself is observable, but the reasoning behind the choice is not. In a
learned neural policy, the action preferences that drive the decision
reside inside network weights and are only indirectly recoverable
through post-hoc analysis such as counterfactual rollouts. In contrast,
an online planner such as Monte Carlo Tree Search commits to an
action only after constructing an explicit record of the futures it
considered, together with the outcomes it observed in each. The tree
is, in a very literal sense, the agent's deliberation.

This paper asks whether that
structural difference between a learned policy and an online planner
has a measurable effect on the quality of the natural-language
explanations each can support. Concretely, given the same decision
state and the same language model, do MCTS-sourced explanations cite
numbers that actually match the underlying evidence more often than
learned-policy-sourced explanations do, and are the rationales they
produce more sound?

Comparing the two paradigms head-to-head is awkward because they
emit different artifacts. A learned policy gives back network
weights and an action distribution; an MCTS planner gives back a
tree of visit counts and child statistics. To put them side by
side, both need to be expressed in the same evidence schema. The
rest of the paper builds that schema and uses it.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Explainable reinforcement learning.}
Milani et al.~\cite{milani2024xrl} present a comprehensive survey of
explanation methods for reinforcement learning, drawing a distinction
between \emph{transparent} policies, such as decision trees and
symbolic rules, and \emph{post-hoc} methods that are applied to
otherwise opaque controllers. Our PPO pipeline falls into the
latter category: counterfactual rollouts
are run after the policy has chosen an action, in order to reconstruct
the set of futures that action implied. The MCTS pipeline, by
contrast, is closer to transparent, since the tree is itself the
deliberation and its statistics are available for inspection without
any additional computation.

\paragraph{Counterfactual explanations for reinforcement learning.}
Gajcin and Dusparic~\cite{gajcin2024cf} propose counterfactual
explanations that perturb state features and observe the resulting
downstream returns, addressing the question ``what would the agent
have done if the state had looked different?''. Amitai et
al.~\cite{amitai2024coviz} focus on the complementary question ``what
would have happened if the agent had taken a different action?'' and
introduce the COViz visualization. The evidence pipeline described in
Section~\ref{sec:approach} operationalizes the latter form concretely,
as forced-first-action rollouts of fixed length, aggregated into
per-action means, success rates, collision rates, and bootstrap
confidence intervals. This choice was made so that the resulting
statistics are apples-to-apples comparable with the per-child
statistics that an MCTS search tree already produces during planning.

\paragraph{MCTS and interpretability.}
Baier and Kaisers~\cite{baier2021explain} argue that the MCTS tree is
a direct substrate for explanation precisely because it records what
the agent considered and what it found. They emphasize visit-count
ratios and per-child value contrasts as the most salient tree
quantities. We adopt these same quantities, together with per-child
empirical success and collision rates instrumented during search, as
the MCTS-side evidence.

\paragraph{Large language models for natural-language explanation.}
Recent work surveyed in~\cite{milani2024xrl} uses large language
models to translate structured evidence about a reinforcement-learning
agent into natural language. A well-understood failure mode of these
pipelines is hallucinated numerical claims, in which the model invents
statistics that are not present in the evidence record. The fidelity
metric defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics} is designed to quantify
exactly that failure mode. Belouadah et
al.~\cite{belouadah2025llm}
benchmark LLM-generated RL explanations on automated fidelity and
soundness metrics under chain-of-thought prompting and MCTS
augmentation, and report that MCTS-augmented prompting improves both.
Our three-metric suite uses the same fidelity and soundness
definitions as that benchmark, and extends it with a post-hoc
inferability check that uses a held-out LLM judge.

\paragraph{Environment and planner.}
The benchmark environment is
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}~\cite{minigrid}, a stochastic
gridworld whose adversarial obstacle dynamics are specifically chosen
to make sparse-reward model-free reinforcement learning difficult. The
MCTS planner follows the classic UCT formulation of Kocsis and
Szepesv\'ari~\cite{kocsis2006uct}, with per-simulation sampling of
the stochastic obstacle transition so that visit counts at the root
converge in expectation over the stochastic futures.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
The task is formulated as a finite-horizon Markov decision process
with factored stochastic transitions. In the notation of Kochenderfer
et al.~\cite{kochenderfer2022adm}, the state, action, transition, and
reward are as follows.

\paragraph{State.} A state is written as
$s = (p_a, d_a, \{p^{(k)}_o\}_{k=1}^{4}, t)$, where
$p_a \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes the agent's grid position,
$d_a \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ denotes its facing direction (east, south,
west, north), each $p^{(k)}_o \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes one of four
obstacle positions, and $t \in \{0, \dots, 256\}$ is the step counter.

\paragraph{Action.} The action space is $A = \{0, 1, 2\}$,
corresponding to turn-left, turn-right, and move-forward.

\paragraph{Transition.} The transition $T(s' \mid s, a)$ factors into
a deterministic agent step, followed by a stochastic per-obstacle
random walk in which each obstacle attempts a uniform move into one
of its four orthogonal neighbors; moves blocked by walls, the goal,
or other obstacles leave the obstacle in place. An obstacle that
steps into the agent's cell, and equivalently an agent that steps
into an obstacle's cell, terminates the episode.

\paragraph{Reward.} The reward function is
\begin{equation}
R(s, a, s') =
\begin{cases}
1 - 0.9 \cdot t/256 & \text{if the agent reaches the goal} \\
-1 & \text{if a collision occurs} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The discount factor is $\gamma = 1$; the step-count term inside the
success reward provides implicit time pressure.

\paragraph{Observability.} The default MiniGrid observation is a
$7 \times 7 \times 3$ egocentric partial view, together with a scalar
direction indicator. Here, all learned-policy variants are trained on
a 14-dimensional flat symbolic encoding of the full
ground-truth state (agent position and direction, plus each obstacle's
position); the MCTS planner plans on the full ground-truth state via
a deep-copied simulator. Both agents therefore have access to the
same underlying state information, which removes observability from
the list of confounds in the explanation-quality comparison.

\paragraph{Scope.} Three scope choices shape what follows. The
deep-RL agent is PPO, not DQN: a value-based baseline collapsed to
a near-stall policy under the raw MiniGrid reward, and switching to
a policy-gradient method was the only way we got a competent
learned agent on this environment. Both agents are evaluated on the
flat symbolic encoding above rather than on the default partial
view, which keeps observability out of the comparison. And the
explanation-quality comparison is run at two operating points,
mismatched and matched task performance, rather than at one; the
contrast between the two is what isolates agent competence from the
evidence-source choice.

\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{PPO Agent (Off-the-shelf)}
PPO is used as the learned-policy agent, as implemented in
Stable-Baselines3~\cite{sb3}. The observation is the
14-dimensional flat symbolic encoding described in
Section~\ref{sec:problem}. The policy network is a three-layer MLP of
width $256 \to 256 \to 128$. The environment is wrapped in a
\texttt{StepPenaltyWrapper} that subtracts a flat $0.01$ per
non-terminal step, and a \texttt{DistanceShapingWrapper} that adds a
potential-based Manhattan-distance shaping term with coefficient
$0.02$. The distance shaping is potential-based and therefore
policy-invariant under discounted-return semantics, and serves to
provide a dense gradient that the on-policy advantage estimator can
follow during early training. PPO is trained for $5{,}000{,}000$
environment steps across sixteen parallel envs, with linear schedules
on the learning rate ($3\!\times\!10^{-4}\!\to\!10^{-5}$) and the
clip range ($0.2\!\to\!0.05$) over the course of training, and a
constant entropy bonus of $0.005$. Training uses the shaped reward;
evaluation is always performed against the unshaped task reward, so
the reported success rate reflects the genuine task performance the
agent achieves under the original MDP.

\subsection{MCTS Planner (From-scratch)}
The MCTS planner implements textbook UCT~\cite{kocsis2006uct} with UCB1 selection at exploration constant
$c = \sqrt{2}$, full-width expansion at the root (the action space is
small enough that no progressive widening is required), rollout under
a greedy-toward-goal heuristic capped at depth 60, and mean-value
backup. Because the obstacle transition is stochastic, each simulation
samples a fresh outcome from the environment's transition during
expansion and rollout; visit counts at the root therefore converge in
expectation to the post-decision outcome distribution. Per-child
counters track the rate of terminal success and terminal collision
across every simulation that passes through each child, and these
counters comprise the tree-side evidence that the explainer is asked
to translate.

\subsection{Counterfactual-rollout Framework}
At each decision state, the counterfactual-rollout
framework forces each legal action at step zero and then follows the
trained PPO policy to termination or a depth cap of 40 steps. In the
main experiments reported below, the process is repeated $N=100$
times per action, with action choices at every subsequent step drawn
\emph{stochastically} from PPO's action distribution rather than
taken as the argmax. The setting yields a genuine sampled estimate of
the policy-induced trajectory distribution from the decision state. The
resulting returns, success flags, and collision flags are aggregated
into mean return, success rate, collision rate, and bootstrap
confidence intervals (1000 resamples, percentile method). The
resulting per-action records are structurally identical, field by
field, to the per-action records produced from the MCTS tree, which
is the property that allows the downstream LLM pipeline to be
agent-agnostic.

\subsection{LLM Explainer}
A \texttt{DecisionRecord}, comprising the state, the
chosen action, the per-action evidence, and a small agent-metadata
block (PPO action probabilities or MCTS root-visit statistics), is
passed to a generator LLM using a versioned system prompt. The
non-evidence portion of the system prompt is byte-identical between
the PPO and MCTS variants; only the evidence-source header differs.
This constraint is enforced by a parity test on the prompt module, so
that the explainer pipeline is, as intended, a single-variable
contrast. The generator returns a JSON object consisting of a
\emph{rationale}, a \emph{counterfactual}, a \emph{confidence} score
on $[0, 1]$, and a list of numerical \emph{claims} suitable for
automated verification. Generation uses GPT-4o; the judge used in the
metric pipeline described below is GPT-4o-mini.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Three per-explanation scores are reported, each aggregated per
evidence source with 95\% percentile-bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{\emph{Fidelity.}} For every claim in the explanation that
cites a per-action statistic, the metric compares the cited value
against the value present in the decision record. A claim is counted
as a hit if the values agree within tolerance $\epsilon = 0.1$. The
score is the fraction of claims that hit.

\textbf{\emph{Soundness.}} Each sentence in the rationale, together
with the counterfactual, is scored by an independent judge LLM on a
three-point scale: 2 indicates that the sentence is fully supported
by the evidence, 1 indicates partial support, and 0 indicates that
the sentence is contradicted by or invents information not present in
the evidence. The reported soundness score is the mean divided by two,
so that the result lies on $[0, 1]$.

\textbf{\emph{Post-hoc inferability.}} Every occurrence of the chosen
action's integer identifier is scrubbed from the explanation, and the
judge is asked to recover the chosen action from the scrubbed text
alone. The reported score is the judge's accuracy across decisions.

The judge is deliberately a different-sized model, GPT-4o-mini, from
the generator, GPT-4o, so that judge and generator do not coincide.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Task Performance}
Table~\ref{tab:taskperf} and Figure~\ref{fig:taskperf} report
success rate over 300 episodes for PPO and over 50 episodes for MCTS.
The random-action baseline succeeds 0.3\% of the time, which confirms
that the environment is non-trivial. The tuned PPO agent described in
Section~\ref{sec:approach} reaches a task-success rate of $1.000$
[95\% CI 1.000, 1.000], a collision rate of $0.000$, and a mean
return of $+0.942$, with a mean episode length of 16.6 steps. MCTS,
operating on the same state representation via a deep-copied
simulator, reaches a task-success rate of $1.000$ [95\% CI 1.000,
1.000] with a mean return of $0.934$ and a mean episode length of
18.8 steps. The two agents are performance-matched on the task; PPO
is marginally faster by $\approx 2$ steps and marginally higher in
mean return due to the step-discount in the goal reward. The
explanation-quality comparison below is therefore between two
equally-competent agents.

\subsection{Explanation Quality}
\label{sec:expquality}
Table~\ref{tab:metrics} and Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} (right panel)
report the three explanation-quality metrics for the
performance-matched comparison, evaluated on 141 PPO decisions and 148
MCTS decisions drawn from ten held-out trajectory seeds. PPO-rollout
evidence produced explanations with a fidelity of 0.924 [0.906,
0.939], a soundness of 0.884 [0.862, 0.906], and a post-hoc
inferability of 0.979 [0.950, 1.000]. MCTS-tree evidence produced
explanations with a fidelity of 0.924 [0.907, 0.940], a soundness of
0.892 [0.872, 0.913], and an inferability of 0.986 [0.966, 1.000].
All three confidence-interval pairs overlap heavily, and the absolute
mean differences are well within sampling noise: $0.000$ on fidelity,
$0.008$ on soundness, $0.007$ on inferability. The headline result
of the performance-matched comparison is therefore that the
two evidence sources support explanations of statistically
indistinguishable quality on every metric we measure.

The contrast against an earlier run of the same pipeline against a
less-competent PPO is informative. Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} (left
panel) reproduces the metrics for the same comparison run when PPO
reached only $0.590$ task-success against an MCTS at $1.000$. Under
mismatched performance, MCTS-tree evidence held a small but
statistically supported advantage on fidelity ($0.928$ vs.~$0.899$,
gap $0.029$) and on soundness ($0.879$ vs.~$0.847$, gap $0.032$),
with inferability already tied. Once PPO is matched to MCTS at
$1.000$ task-success, all three gaps collapse to within sampling
noise (right panel). The simplest interpretation consistent with
the two runs is that the apparent MCTS-tree advantage in the
mismatched run was a \emph{competence confound} rather than a
structural property of the search-tree-vs-learned-policy paradigms:
a less-competent learned policy produces noisier counterfactual
rollouts, and the LLM has correspondingly more difficulty citing
those numbers within the fidelity tolerance. Once the policy is
competent its rollouts are as faithful and sound an evidence source
as the deliberation tree itself.

\subsection{Qualitative Example}
\label{sec:qualitative}
Figure~\ref{fig:qualex} reproduces verbatim the two explanations

chose \emph{different} actions at this state, PPO selecting

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\textbf{\emph{State-distribution asymmetry.}} Although PPO and MCTS
are now performance-matched at $1.000$ task-success, the
\emph{trajectories} the two agents follow are not literally identical.
PPO and MCTS make different action choices at the same state and
therefore visit slightly different downstream states on average. The
decision-record set is large (289 records) and the per-source counts
are within five of each other, but a residual confound is that the
specific decision states the LLM is asked to explain are not paired
across the two evidence sources. A paired analysis on the same
literal state set is left as future work.

\textbf{\emph{Rollout sample size.}} Counterfactual rollouts use
$N=100$ stochastic samples per action. The bootstrap confidence
intervals on per-action success and collision rates remain in the
neighborhood of $\pm 0.10$ at $N=100$, which is the same order as
the LLM fidelity tolerance ($\epsilon = 0.1$). A larger Monte Carlo
budget would tighten those intervals further, but we do not expect
it to change the qualitative finding because the fidelity numbers
for the two sources are already equal to three decimal places.

\textbf{\emph{Judge-model bias.}} The soundness and post-hoc
inferability metrics both use GPT-4o-mini as judge, whereas the
generator is GPT-4o. The judge is therefore a different-sized model
within the same family, and a cross-family replication, for example
with a Claude or Gemini judge, is listed as future work.

\textbf{\emph{Inferability metric leak.}} The post-hoc inferability
test scrubs the chosen action's integer identifier from the
explanation, but the verbal action name (\texttt{move\_forward},
\texttt{turn\_right}) still appears in the rationale. The judge can
therefore recover the action from the verbal name alone, which
inflates inferability scores and reduces the metric's discriminative
power; this likely explains why both sources score above $0.97$ in
the matched run. A stricter version of the test would scrub both the
integer identifier and the verbal name from the rationale before
showing it to the judge.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper has presented an evaluation of two evidence sources for
natural-language explanations of reinforcement-learning agents:
post-hoc counterfactual rollouts drawn stochastically from a learned
neural policy at $N=100$ samples per action, and the search tree of
a Monte Carlo Tree Search planner at 500 simulations per decision.
Across 289 decision states drawn from two performance-matched agents
on \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} (PPO 1.000 task-success,
MCTS 1.000 task-success), the two pipelines produced explanations of
statistically indistinguishable quality on every metric measured: a
fidelity of 0.924 for both sources, a soundness of 0.884 versus
0.892, and an inferability of 0.979 versus 0.986. None of the
three metric gaps is statistically supported.

This is a mildly negative result with respect to the original
hypothesis that the search tree should confer a structural
explanation advantage over a learned policy. The contrast against
the earlier mismatched run is what makes it informative: at PPO
$0.590$ versus MCTS $1.000$, MCTS-tree evidence held a small but
statistically supported lead on fidelity ($0.029$) and soundness
($0.032$). Once both agents are matched at $1.000$, the gaps
collapse. The simplest reading is that the earlier MCTS-tree
advantage was a competence confound: a less-competent learned policy
produces noisier counterfactual rollouts that an LLM has more
difficulty citing faithfully. Once the policy is competent, its
rollouts are as faithful and sound an evidence source as the
deliberation tree itself, and the suboptimality of the underlying
agent is what bounds the signal available for any post-hoc
counterfactual analysis.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
We see four natural extensions. A paired analysis on the literal same
decision-state set, rather than two trajectory sets that differ in
the agents' action choices, would remove the residual
state-distribution confound noted in the threats to validity. A pilot
human study would validate the automated metrics against human
judgements of clarity, plausibility, and helpfulness; a chatbot
interface suitable for such a study is already scaffolded. A
cross-family LLM replication, with a judge drawn from a different
model family than the generator, would quantify the contribution of
judge-model bias to the soundness numbers reported above. A more
ambitious extension would replicate the null result on a harder
environment in which neither paradigm reaches $1.000$ task-success,
to test whether the equivalence of the two evidence sources holds at
an intermediate competence level or only at the extremes.
