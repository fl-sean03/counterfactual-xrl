\begin{abstract}
This paper evaluates the relative explainability of online planning and
deep reinforcement learning when each agent is asked to justify its
own decisions. A proximal-policy-optimisation (PPO) agent and a
from-scratch Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) planner are operated on the
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} environment. For each agent,
per-action evidence is extracted at decision states, counterfactual
Monte Carlo rollouts in the PPO case and the search tree in the MCTS
case, and passed through an identically-formatted large-language-model
explainer that produces a natural-language rationale, a counterfactual,
a confidence, and a list of verifiable numerical claims. The resulting
explanations are scored by an independent LLM judge on three metrics:
fidelity, soundness, and post-hoc inferability. Across 262 decision
records from ten held-out seeds (121 PPO decisions and 141 MCTS
decisions), with PPO counterfactual rollouts generated by stochastic
sampling from the policy distribution at $N=100$ independently seeded
samples per action, MCTS-tree evidence produced explanations with
higher numerical fidelity, 0.937 [95\% CI 0.915, 0.956] versus
0.901 [0.875, 0.924] for PPO-rollout evidence. PPO-rollout evidence,
however, produced higher judge-rated soundness, 0.847 [0.820, 0.872]
versus 0.788 [0.763, 0.814] for MCTS. Post-hoc inferability was
essentially tied at 0.975 for PPO and 0.965 for MCTS. The central
finding is therefore more nuanced than a simple planning-versus-learning
ranking: the tree gives the language model cleaner numeric evidence to
cite, but the rollout explanations were judged as better supported in
their qualitative reasoning on this run.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
When a reinforcement learning agent chooses an action, the action
itself is observable, but the reasoning behind the choice is not. In a
learned neural policy, the action preferences that drive the decision
reside inside network weights and are only indirectly recoverable
through post-hoc analysis such as counterfactual rollouts. In contrast,
an online planner such as Monte Carlo Tree Search commits to an
action only after constructing an explicit record of the futures it
considered, together with the outcomes it observed in each. The tree
is, in a very literal sense, the agent's deliberation.

This paper asks, following the framing of Prof.\ Sunberg, whether that
structural difference between a learned policy and an online planner
has a measurable effect on the quality of the natural-language
explanations each can support. Concretely, when each agent is evaluated
on held-out trajectories with the same language-model pipeline, do
MCTS-sourced explanations cite numbers that actually match the
underlying evidence more often than learned-policy-sourced
explanations do, and are the rationales they produce more sound?

To address this question, the present authors construct a shared
evidence-to-explanation pipeline and apply it to two agents on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, a stochastic gridworld whose
adversarial obstacle dynamics are well-suited to separating competent
agents from incompetent ones. The deep-reinforcement-learning side is
represented by a PPO policy, which reaches a 0.590 task-success rate
over 300 held-out episodes. The planning side is represented by a
from-scratch UCT planner, which reaches 1.000 success over 50 episodes.
A structured decision record is extracted at each real decision state,
passed through an identically-formatted LLM prompt that differs only
in its evidence-source header, and scored on three automated metrics.

The empirical finding, obtained over 262 decision records with PPO
counterfactual rollouts at $N=100$ independently seeded stochastic
samples per action, is that MCTS-tree evidence yields a modest fidelity
advantage over PPO-rollout evidence (0.937 versus 0.901, 95\%
confidence intervals nearly disjoint), while PPO-rollout evidence
yields a soundness advantage (0.847 versus 0.788). Post-hoc
inferability is essentially tied (0.975 for PPO and 0.965 for MCTS).
A separate diagnostic run with an earlier implementation, in which PPO
rollouts used only $N=20$ deterministic argmax samples, gave a larger
fidelity gap; the current run shows that improving the Monte Carlo
estimator materially narrows the numerical-fidelity advantage of the
tree.

Also, as a preliminary diagnostic, the present authors observed that a
value-based baseline (DQN) on this environment collapses into a
degenerate stall policy under the raw reward structure, and that its
counterfactual rollouts therefore provide little signal to the
explainer. This observation is reported in the ablation of
Section~\ref{sec:dqn-ablation} and motivates the choice of PPO as the
deep-reinforcement-learning agent in the main comparison.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The present work contributes: (1) a from-scratch MCTS planner for
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} that emits per-decision
tree-statistics in a format suitable for downstream explanation;
(2) a policy-agnostic counterfactual-rollout framework that produces
learned-policy-side evidence in a structurally identical format to the
MCTS tree; (3) three automated metrics, fidelity, soundness, and
post-hoc inferability, implemented with bootstrap confidence intervals;
(4) an empirical comparison of the two paradigms on 262 held-out
decision records, using a real LLM generator and a held-out LLM judge;
and (5) a diagnostic ablation on a value-based baseline showing that
model-free value learning collapses into a degenerate stall policy
under the raw MiniGrid reward, and that this collapse in turn degrades
the evidence available for explanation.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Explainable reinforcement learning.}
Milani et al.~\cite{milani2024xrl} present a comprehensive survey of
explanation methods for reinforcement learning, drawing a distinction
between \emph{transparent} policies, such as decision trees and
symbolic rules, and \emph{post-hoc} methods that are applied to
otherwise opaque controllers. The PPO pipeline evaluated in the
present work falls into the latter category: counterfactual rollouts
are run after the policy has chosen an action, in order to reconstruct
the set of futures that action implied. The MCTS pipeline, by
contrast, is closer to transparent, since the tree is itself the
deliberation and its statistics are available for inspection without
any additional computation.

\paragraph{Counterfactual explanations for reinforcement learning.}
Gajcin and Dusparic~\cite{gajcin2024cf} propose counterfactual
explanations that perturb state features and observe the resulting
downstream returns, addressing the question ``what would the agent
have done if the state had looked different?''. Amitai et
al.~\cite{amitai2024coviz} focus on the complementary question ``what
would have happened if the agent had taken a different action?'' and
introduce the COViz visualisation. The evidence pipeline described in
Section~\ref{sec:approach} operationalises the latter form concretely,
as forced-first-action rollouts of fixed length, aggregated into
per-action means, success rates, collision rates, and bootstrap
confidence intervals. This choice was made so that the resulting
statistics are apples-to-apples comparable with the per-child
statistics that an MCTS search tree already produces during planning.

\paragraph{MCTS and interpretability.}
Baier and Kaisers~\cite{baier2021explain} argue that the MCTS tree is
a direct substrate for explanation precisely because it records what
the agent considered and what it found. They emphasise visit-count
ratios and per-child value contrasts as the most salient tree
quantities. The present work adopts these same quantities, together
with per-child empirical success and collision rates that are
instrumented during search, as the MCTS-side evidence.

\paragraph{Large language models for natural-language explanation.}
Recent work surveyed in~\cite{milani2024xrl} uses large language
models to translate structured evidence about a reinforcement-learning
agent into natural language. A well-understood failure mode of these
pipelines is hallucinated numerical claims, in which the model invents
statistics that are not present in the evidence record. The fidelity
metric defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics} is designed to quantify
exactly that failure mode. The three-metric suite in the present work
combines this numerical-fidelity check with a judge-rated soundness
score and a post-hoc inferability check that asks whether a held-out
judge can recover the chosen action from the explanation alone.

\paragraph{Environment and planner.}
The benchmark environment used in the present study is
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}~\cite{minigrid}, a stochastic
gridworld whose adversarial obstacle dynamics are specifically chosen
to make sparse-reward model-free reinforcement learning difficult. The
MCTS planner follows the classic UCT formulation of Kocsis and
Szepesv\'ari~\cite{kocsis2006uct}, with per-simulation sampling of
the stochastic obstacle transition so that visit counts at the root
converge in expectation over the stochastic futures.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
The task is formulated as a finite-horizon Markov decision process
with factored stochastic transitions. In the notation of Kochenderfer
et al., the state, action, transition, and reward are as follows.

\paragraph{State.} A state is written as
$s = (p_a, d_a, \{p^{(k)}_o\}_{k=1}^{4}, t)$, where
$p_a \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes the agent's grid position,
$d_a \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ denotes its facing direction (east, south,
west, north), each $p^{(k)}_o \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes one of four
obstacle positions, and $t \in \{0, \dots, 256\}$ is the step counter.

\paragraph{Action.} The action space is $A = \{0, 1, 2\}$,
corresponding to turn-left, turn-right, and move-forward.

\paragraph{Transition.} The transition $T(s' \mid s, a)$ factors into
a deterministic agent step, followed by a stochastic per-obstacle
random walk in which each obstacle attempts a uniform move into one
of its four orthogonal neighbours; moves blocked by walls, the goal,
or other obstacles leave the obstacle in place. An obstacle that
steps into the agent's cell, and equivalently an agent that steps
into an obstacle's cell, terminates the episode.

\paragraph{Reward.} The reward function is
\[
R(s, a, s') =
\begin{cases}
1 - 0.9t/256 & \text{if the agent reaches the goal} \\
-1 & \text{if a collision occurs} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]
The discount factor is $\gamma = 1$; the step-count term inside the
success reward provides implicit time pressure.

\paragraph{Observability.} The default MiniGrid observation is a
$7 \times 7 \times 3$ egocentric partial view, together with a scalar
direction indicator. The main PPO agent and the symbolic DQN controls
are trained on a 14-dimensional flat symbolic encoding of the full
ground-truth state (agent position and direction, plus each obstacle's
position); the image-DQN ablation keeps the default partial image
observation. The MCTS planner plans on the full ground-truth state via
a deep-copied simulator. Thus, the main PPO-versus-MCTS explanation
comparison removes observability as a confound, while the DQN ablation
also reports the partial-observation failure mode.

\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{PPO Agent (Off-the-shelf)}
\textbf{Setup.} The deep-reinforcement-learning agent is PPO, as
implemented in Stable-Baselines3~\cite{sb3}. The observation is the
14-dimensional flat symbolic encoding described in
Section~\ref{sec:problem}. The policy network is a two-layer MLP of
width $128 \to 128$. The environment is wrapped in a
\texttt{StepPenaltyWrapper} that subtracts a flat $0.01$ per
non-terminal step; the decision to introduce this penalty was made
because, as reported in Section~\ref{sec:dqn-ablation}, the raw
MiniGrid reward admits a zero-return stall policy as a local optimum,
and the step penalty removes this degenerate equilibrium. PPO is
trained for $500{,}000$ environment steps across eight parallel
envs, with standard hyperparameters
(\texttt{n\_steps}=2048, \texttt{batch\_size}=64, \texttt{n\_epochs}=10,
\texttt{gamma}=0.99, \texttt{clip\_range}=0.2,
\texttt{ent\_coef}=0.01). Full hyperparameters are committed to
\texttt{configs/ppo\_baseline.yaml}. Training uses the shaped reward;
evaluation is always performed against the unshaped task reward.

\subsection{MCTS Planner (From-scratch)}
\textbf{Setup.} The MCTS planner implements textbook
UCT~\cite{kocsis2006uct} with UCB1 selection at exploration constant
$c = \sqrt{2}$, full-width expansion at the root (the action space is
small enough that no progressive widening is required), rollout under
a greedy-toward-goal heuristic capped at depth 60, and mean-value
backup. Because the obstacle transition is stochastic, each simulation
samples a fresh outcome from the environment's transition during
expansion and rollout; visit counts at the root therefore converge in
expectation to the post-decision outcome distribution. Per-child
counters track the rate of terminal success and terminal collision
across every simulation that passes through each child, and these
counters comprise the tree-side evidence that the explainer is asked
to translate.

\subsection{Counterfactual-rollout Framework}
\textbf{Setup.} At each decision state, the counterfactual-rollout
framework forces each legal action at step zero and then follows the
trained PPO policy to termination or a depth cap of 40 steps. In the
main experiments reported below, the process is repeated $N=100$
times per action, with action choices at every subsequent step drawn
\emph{stochastically} from PPO's action distribution rather than
taken as the argmax. Each cloned simulator branch is reseeded before
the forced action so that the moving-obstacle futures are independent
Monte Carlo samples rather than repeated copies of the same environment
RNG state. This configuration matches the Monte Carlo specification in
the original proposal ($\geq 100$ rollouts per available action) and
yields a sampled estimate of the policy-induced trajectory distribution
from the decision state. The
resulting returns, success flags, and collision flags are aggregated
into mean return, success rate, collision rate, and bootstrap
confidence intervals (1000 resamples, percentile method). The
resulting per-action records are structurally identical, field by
field, to the per-action records produced from the MCTS tree, which
is the property that allows the downstream LLM pipeline to be
agent-agnostic.

\subsection{LLM Explainer}
\textbf{Setup.} A \texttt{DecisionRecord}, comprising the state, the
chosen action, the per-action evidence, and a small agent-metadata
block (PPO action probabilities or MCTS root-visit statistics), is
passed to a generator LLM using a versioned system prompt. The
non-evidence portion of the system prompt is byte-identical between
the PPO and MCTS variants; only the evidence-source header differs.
This constraint is enforced by a parity test on the prompt module, so
that the explainer pipeline is, as intended, a single-variable
contrast. The generator returns a JSON object consisting of a
\emph{rationale}, a \emph{counterfactual}, a \emph{confidence} score
on $[0, 1]$, and a list of numerical \emph{claims} suitable for
automated verification. Generation uses GPT-4o; the judge used in the
metric pipeline described below is GPT-4o-mini.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Three per-explanation scores are reported, each aggregated per
evidence source with 95\% percentile-bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{\emph{Fidelity.}} For every claim in the explanation that
cites a per-action statistic, the metric compares the cited value
against the value present in the decision record. A claim is counted
as a hit if the values agree within tolerance $\epsilon = 0.1$. The
score is the fraction of claims that hit.

\textbf{\emph{Soundness.}} Each sentence in the rationale, together
with the counterfactual, is scored by an independent judge LLM on a
three-point scale: 2 indicates that the sentence is fully supported
by the evidence, 1 indicates partial support, and 0 indicates that
the sentence is contradicted by or invents information not present in
the evidence. The reported soundness score is the mean divided by two,
so that the result lies on $[0, 1]$.

\textbf{\emph{Post-hoc inferability.}} Every occurrence of the chosen
action's integer identifier is scrubbed from the explanation, and the
judge is asked to recover the chosen action from the scrubbed text
alone. The reported score is the judge's accuracy across decisions.

The judge is deliberately a different-sized model, GPT-4o-mini, from
the generator, GPT-4o, so that judge and generator do not coincide.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Task Performance}
Table~\ref{tab:taskperf} and Figure~\ref{fig:taskperf} report
success rate over 300 episodes for PPO and over 50 episodes for MCTS.
The random-action baseline succeeds 0.3\% of the time, which confirms
that the environment is non-trivial. PPO reaches a task-success rate
of 0.590 [95\% CI 0.537, 0.643], a collision rate of 0.410, and a
mean return of $+0.115$, with a mean episode length of 24.5 steps.
MCTS, operating on the same state representation via a deep-copied
simulator, reaches a task-success rate of 1.000 [95\% CI 1.000, 1.000]
with a mean return of 0.936 and a mean episode length of 18.3 steps.
MCTS therefore outperforms PPO in absolute terms, but both agents are
functional and both exceed the random baseline by a wide margin. As
such, the explanation-quality comparison reported below is between
two competent agents rather than between a competent agent and a
degenerate one.

\subsection{Explanation Quality}
\label{sec:expquality}
Table~\ref{tab:metrics} and Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} report the three
explanation-quality metrics, evaluated on 121 PPO decisions and 141
MCTS decisions drawn from ten held-out trajectory seeds. MCTS-tree
evidence produced explanations with a fidelity of 0.937, as compared
to 0.901 for the PPO-rollout pipeline. The confidence intervals on
fidelity nearly separate, supporting the claim that the tree records
give the language model cleaner numerical values to cite. The
soundness result points the other direction: PPO-rollout evidence
scores 0.847, compared to 0.788 for MCTS-tree evidence. Post-hoc
inferability is essentially tied, 0.975 for PPO and 0.965 for MCTS,
meaning both explanation types usually carry enough signal for a judge
to recover the chosen action from the scrubbed rationale.

The split between fidelity and soundness is informative. The MCTS
search tree uses UCB1 selection to preferentially expand promising
actions, so after 500 simulations the chosen or near-chosen action
often has substantially more visits than dominated actions; the
empirical statistics the LLM is asked to cite can therefore round
cleanly to values present in the record. PPO counterfactual rollouts,
by contrast, allocate the full $N=100$ budget uniformly across the
three actions, producing a flatter evidence table and slightly noisier
per-action numerical estimates. This difference plausibly explains the
tree's higher numerical fidelity. However, the judge-rated soundness
metric evaluates sentence-level support, not only exact numerical
matching. On this run, the PPO explanations were judged as more
conservative and better supported in their qualitative framing, while
some MCTS explanations made stronger causal claims from tree statistics
than the judge accepted. The result is therefore not a blanket win for
online planning; it is a more specific claim that the search tree helps
with exact numerical citation, while rollout evidence can still support
sound natural-language rationales.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\textbf{\emph{Task-performance and trajectory asymmetry.}} PPO and
MCTS are not fully performance-matched: MCTS scores 1.00 task success
to PPO's 0.59. Both agents are, however, competent relative to the
random baseline (0.003), and the explanation comparison is therefore
between two functioning agents rather than between a functioning agent
and a degenerate one. A residual effect of the remaining performance
gap is that decision states sampled from PPO trajectories are more
likely to be near-collision states, because PPO fails more often than
MCTS does; this may systematically differ from the states sampled from
MCTS trajectories. The trajectories also diverge after early decisions,
so the 262 explanation records should be read as a same-environment
comparison rather than a fully paired same-state comparison.

\textbf{\emph{Rollout sample size.}} Counterfactual rollouts use 100
independently seeded samples per action. This matches the proposal's
Monte Carlo budget and is sufficient for the present comparison, but it
is still finite: empirical success rates near 0.5 have a standard error
of about 0.05 per action before accounting for downstream policy
stochasticity. Increasing the rollout count would further stabilize the
PPO evidence, though at a linear compute cost.

\textbf{\emph{Judge-model bias.}} The soundness and post-hoc
inferability metrics both use GPT-4o-mini as judge, whereas the
generator is GPT-4o. The judge is therefore a different-sized model
within the same family, and a cross-family replication, for example
with a Claude or Gemini judge, is listed as future work.

\subsection{Value-based Ablation: Why DQN Was Cut}
\label{sec:dqn-ablation}
As a diagnostic ablation, three DQN variants were trained on the same
environment: one on the default partial-observation image, one on the
flat symbolic state, and one with a potential-based distance-shaping
reward. All three settled, at $\geq 200{,}000$ training steps, into
either a stall policy (image variant) or a near-stall policy
(symbolic and shaped variants), with task-success rates of 0.010,
0.000, and 0.000 respectively (see Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}). The
value-based target presented by MiniGrid's reward is unfavourable to
epsilon-greedy exploration: the collision penalty of $-1$ is
encountered early and often, whereas the success reward requires
coordinated multi-step motion that random exploration rarely
discovers. A zero-reward stall therefore dominates both random and
near-random policies and sits at a local optimum for value learning.
The present authors attempted to break this equilibrium with a flat
$-0.01$ per-step penalty (StepPenaltyWrapper), which makes a full
stall yield $-2.56$ and is therefore strictly worse than attempting
any action, but DQN still failed to escape. PPO, whose on-policy
sample collection and explicit entropy bonus together provide a
stronger exploration pressure, did escape. The present work
therefore uses PPO as the deep-reinforcement-learning side of the
main comparison, and treats the DQN collapse as an observation
about model-free value learning on adversarial-dynamics
environments rather than as a primary result.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper has presented an evaluation of two evidence sources for
natural-language explanations of reinforcement-learning agents:
post-hoc counterfactual rollouts drawn stochastically from a learned
neural policy at $N=100$ samples per action, and the search tree of
a Monte Carlo Tree Search planner at 500 simulations per decision.
Across 262 held-out decision records drawn from
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, the MCTS-tree pipeline
produced explanations that were more faithful to the underlying
numbers (0.937 versus 0.901), while PPO-rollout explanations were
judged sounder at the sentence level (0.847 versus 0.788). Post-hoc
inferability was essentially tied (0.975 for PPO versus 0.965 for
MCTS). A separate diagnostic run in which the PPO-side rollouts used
only $N=20$ samples and took the argmax at every rollout step produced
a larger apparent fidelity gap; most of that larger gap was a
sampling-noise artefact of the weaker Monte Carlo setup, not a
paradigm difference.

Two caveats condition this result. Firstly, PPO and MCTS are not
fully performance-matched on the task: MCTS scores 1.00 task-success
to PPO's 0.59, so decision states sampled from PPO trajectories are
more likely to be near-collision states than those sampled from MCTS
trajectories, which may bias the comparison in ways the present
experimental design does not remove. They are also not fully paired
same-state explanations after the trajectories diverge. Secondly, the
judge LLM is a different-sized model within the same family as the
generator, so a cross-family replication is required before the
soundness numbers can be taken as robust to judge-model bias.

Simply put, the results of the present work suggest that, when the
learned policy is sampled at a Monte Carlo budget comparable to the
tree's simulation budget, the structural difference between a search
tree and a learned policy translates most clearly into a numerical
fidelity advantage for the tree-based evidence, not into a uniform
advantage across all explanation-quality metrics. The plausible source
of this fidelity gap is the UCB1 selection rule inside MCTS, which
concentrates tree samples on the decision-relevant action and therefore
reports numerically tighter statistics for that action than flat
rollout allocation can. The PPO explanations' stronger soundness score
suggests that conservative rollout summaries can still be easier for a
judge to verify as qualitative rationales. A secondary finding,
documented in the ablation of Section~\ref{sec:dqn-ablation}, is
that model-free value learning on this environment collapses into a
degenerate stall policy under the raw MiniGrid reward, and that such
a collapse also degrades the evidence available for subsequent
explanation; the suboptimality of the underlying agent therefore
directly limits the signal available for any post-hoc counterfactual
analysis.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
The present authors see three natural extensions. Firstly, a
performance-matched comparison, obtained by either further improving
PPO (e.g., through longer training, a curriculum over grid size, or
policy-distillation from MCTS) or deliberately downgrading MCTS
(e.g., by capping its simulation budget until its task success
matches PPO's), would isolate the contribution of paradigm from that
of competence. Secondly, a pilot human study, following the
suggestion of Prof.\ Sunberg on the proposal, would validate the
automated metrics against human judgements of clarity, plausibility,
and helpfulness; a chatbot interface suitable for such a study has
already been scaffolded in \texttt{scripts/chat.py}. Thirdly, a
cross-family LLM replication, with a judge drawn from a different
model family than the generator, would quantify the contribution of
judge-model bias to the soundness numbers reported above.
