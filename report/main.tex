\begin{abstract}
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice]
We investigate whether online planning with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
produces higher-quality explanations than post-hoc counterfactual analysis
of a deep Q-network (DQN). Both agents are trained or planned on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, a stochastic grid MDP, and
evaluated under a shared evidence-to-explanation pipeline: structured
per-action statistics are generated from each agent's decision process
(counterfactual rollouts for DQN, the search tree for MCTS), passed
through an LLM-based natural-language explainer, and scored on fidelity,
soundness, and post-hoc inferability. Our central question, following
Prof. Sunberg's framing: is the \emph{suboptimality} of an online
planner more explainable than the suboptimality of a learned policy,
specifically because the planner's ``training data'' is recorded in the
tree?
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice. The findings below are real; the
%         phrasing is a starting point.]
When a reinforcement learning agent chooses an action, we usually know
\emph{what} it chose but not \emph{why}. The action-value estimates
behind a deep Q-network's decision live inside neural network weights,
so its reasoning is opaque and only indirectly recoverable via post-hoc
methods like counterfactual rollouts. Online planners such as
Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) are structurally different: by the time
they commit to an action, they have already written down — in the form
of a search tree — a record of which futures they simulated and what
happened in each. The tree \emph{is} the agent's deliberation.

This paper asks, following Prof.\ Sunberg's framing, whether that
structural difference has an observable effect on explanation quality.
Concretely: given the same decision state and the same language model,
do MCTS-sourced explanations cite numbers that actually match the
evidence more often than DQN-sourced explanations do?

We compare the two paradigms on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, a stochastic gridworld, under
a shared evidence-to-explanation pipeline: a structured
\texttt{DecisionRecord} is extracted from each agent (counterfactual
rollouts for DQN, the search tree for MCTS), passed through an
identically-formatted LLM prompt (only the ``evidence source'' header
differs), and scored on three metrics --- fidelity, soundness, and
post-hoc inferability --- with bootstrap confidence intervals.

Our empirical finding is that MCTS-tree evidence yields more than twice
the fidelity of DQN-rollout evidence (0.93 vs.\ 0.44; 95\% CIs
non-overlapping) and substantially higher soundness (0.89 vs.\ 0.76),
while post-hoc inferability is statistically tied ($\sim$0.95 for both). A plausible
mechanism, supported by our trained DQN's settling into a degenerate
stall policy, is that \emph{the suboptimality of the underlying agent
directly degrades the quality of the evidence available for
explanation}. When DQN's counterfactual rollouts all yield zero return,
the LLM is asked to justify a choice against an evidence row with no
signal in it.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A from-scratch MCTS planner for
        \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} with a tree-dump format
        designed for downstream explanation.
  \item A policy-agnostic counterfactual rollout framework producing
        DQN-side evidence in a format structurally identical to the
        MCTS tree statistics.
  \item Three automated metrics for LLM-generated explanations:
        fidelity, soundness, and post-hoc inferability.
  \item Empirical results comparing the two paradigms on matched
        decision states.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice; citations verified except [belouadah]]

\paragraph{Explainable reinforcement learning.}
Milani et al.~\cite{milani2024xrl} survey explanation methods for RL,
distinguishing \emph{transparent} policies (e.g., decision trees,
symbolic rules) from \emph{post-hoc} methods applied to opaque
controllers. Our DQN pipeline is post-hoc: counterfactual rollouts are
run \emph{after} the policy has chosen an action, to reconstruct the
set of futures that action implied. Our MCTS pipeline is closer to
transparent: the tree \emph{is} the deliberation, and its statistics
are available for inspection without any additional computation.

\paragraph{Counterfactual explanations for RL.}
Gajcin and Dusparic~\cite{gajcin2024cf} propose counterfactual
explanations that perturb state features and observe downstream
returns, addressing ``what would the agent have done if the state
looked different?''. Amitai et al.~\cite{amitai2024coviz} focus on the
complementary question ``what would happen if the agent had taken a
different action?'' and introduce the COViz visualization. Our
evidence pipeline operationalizes the latter form concretely as
forced-first-action rollouts of fixed length $N=20$, aggregated into
per-action means, success rates, collision rates, and bootstrap CIs.
This gives an apples-to-apples comparison with the per-child statistics
that an MCTS search tree already produces during planning.

\paragraph{MCTS interpretability.}
Baier and Kaisers~\cite{baier2021explain} argue that MCTS's tree is a
direct substrate for explanation because it records what the agent
considered and what it found. They emphasize visit-count ratios and
per-child value contrasts; we use these same quantities, plus the
empirical success/collision rates we instrument during search, as our
MCTS-side evidence.

\paragraph{LLMs for natural-language explanation.}
Recent work (surveyed in \cite{milani2024xrl}) uses large language
models to translate structured evidence about an RL agent into natural
language explanations. A common failure mode is hallucinated statistics
--- the LLM inventing numbers not present in the evidence. Our
fidelity metric is designed to quantify exactly that failure mode.
Belouadah et al.~\cite{belouadah2025llm}\footnote{Citation to be
verified; may be replaced with Hoffman et al.\ 2018 on XAI metrics.}
propose evaluating LLM-generated RL explanations with automated
fidelity and plausibility scores; our three-metric suite
(Section~\ref{sec:metrics}) extends this framework with a post-hoc
inferability check using a held-out LLM judge.

\paragraph{The environment and planner.}
We use \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}~\cite{minigrid}, a
stochastic gridworld where the adversarial obstacle dynamics are
specifically chosen to make sparse-reward model-free RL difficult.
Our MCTS follows the classic UCT formulation of Kocsis and
Szepesv\'ari~\cite{kocsis2006uct}, with per-simulation obstacle
transition sampling so visit counts at the root converge in expectation
over the stochastic futures.

\section{Problem Formulation}
% Copy the authoritative formulation from docs/problem_formulation.md.
% Include S, A, T, R definitions verbatim.

We formulate \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} as a finite-horizon
MDP with factored stochastic transitions.

\paragraph{State} $s = (p_a, d_a, \{p^{(k)}_o\}_{k=1..4}, t)$, where
$p_a \in [1,6]^2$ is the agent's position, $d_a \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ its
direction (east/south/west/north), $p^{(k)}_o \in [1,6]^2$ each
obstacle's position, and $t$ the step counter.

\paragraph{Action} $A = \{0, 1, 2\}$ = \{turn left, turn right, move
forward\}.

\paragraph{Transition} $T(s' \mid s, a)$ factors into a deterministic
agent step followed by a stochastic per-obstacle random walk (each
obstacle attempts a uniform move to one of its 4 neighbors; blocked
moves leave the obstacle in place). An obstacle moving into the agent
(or vice versa) terminates the episode.

\paragraph{Reward} $R$ is $+ (1 - 0.9\,t/256)$ on reaching the goal,
$-1$ on collision, and $0$ otherwise. The discount factor is $\gamma =
1$; the step-count term inside the success reward provides implicit
time pressure.

\paragraph{Observability.} The MiniGrid default observation is a $7
\times 7 \times 3$ egocentric partial view plus a direction scalar.
The DQN is trained on this observation; MCTS plans on the full state
via a deep-copied simulator. We address this asymmetry in
\S\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{DQN Baseline (off-the-shelf)}
% [DRAFT — details in src/xrl/agents/dqn.py and configs/dqn_*.yaml]
We use Stable-Baselines3~\cite{sb3} DQN with a two-layer MLP
($256 \to 256$) policy. Observations are either flattened egocentric
image or the full symbolic state (14-dimensional). Hyperparameters are
in \texttt{configs/dqn\_*.yaml}.

\subsection{MCTS Planner (from scratch)}
Our MCTS implements textbook UCT~\cite{kocsis2006uct}: UCB1 selection
($c = \sqrt{2}$), full-width expansion at the root, rollouts under a
greedy-toward-goal heuristic policy (capped at depth 60), mean-value
backup, sampled-transition handling for stochastic obstacle dynamics.
Per-child counters record the rate of terminal success and collision
across every simulation passing through them — this is the tree-side
evidence.

\subsection{Counterfactual Framework}
For a decision state, ``counterfactual rollouts'' force each legal
action at step 0 then follow the trained DQN policy to termination. We
repeat $N = 100$ times per action and compute mean return, success
rate, collision rate, and mean steps-to-end with 95\% bootstrap
confidence intervals. The resulting \texttt{ActionStats} are
byte-identically structured whether the source is rollouts (DQN) or the
search tree (MCTS).

\subsection{LLM Explanation Pipeline}
A \texttt{DecisionRecord} (state + per-action stats + agent metadata)
is passed to Claude~\cite{anthropic} with a versioned system prompt.
The non-evidence portion of the system prompt is byte-identical between
the DQN and MCTS variants (enforced by a test); only the evidence
source header differs. Claude returns a JSON object with
\texttt{rationale}, \texttt{counterfactual}, \texttt{confidence}, and a
list of numerical \texttt{claims} suitable for automated verification.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Three per-explanation scores, aggregated per agent with bootstrap CIs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fidelity}: fraction of numerical claims in the
        explanation whose cited value matches the evidence within
        tolerance $\epsilon = 0.1$.
  \item \textbf{Soundness}: a separate LLM judge (Haiku 4.5) scores
        each rationale sentence on $\{0, 1, 2\}$ for support by the
        evidence. Reported as mean / 2.
  \item \textbf{Post-hoc inferability}: the judge sees the explanation
        with action numbers scrubbed and predicts the chosen action.
        Accuracy across decisions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Explanation quality}
\label{sec:expquality}
Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} and Table~\ref{tab:metrics} compare the three
metrics across evidence sources, using 45 matched decisions per agent.
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice; verify numbers.]
MCTS-tree-sourced explanations attain more than twice the fidelity of
DQN-rollout-sourced ones (0.93 vs 0.44) and substantially higher
soundness (0.89 vs 0.76). Post-hoc inferability is essentially tied
(0.96 vs 0.95; CIs overlap): both explanations carry enough signal for
a judge to recover the chosen action, but only MCTS explanations cite
numbers that actually match the evidence roughly twice as often.

We attribute the fidelity gap to the structure of each evidence source.
The MCTS search tree records per-action visit counts and empirical
success/collision rates from simulations \emph{that were actually
executed during deliberation}. In contrast, our DQN agent settled into
a degenerate stall policy (Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}), so counterfactual
rollouts from a decision state produce near-zero signal (nearly all
actions yield 0 return at the rollout-step cap). The LLM is asked to
justify a choice whose evidence row is essentially ``no outcome for any
action,'' and the explanation necessarily reaches for values that the
record does not actually contain.

\subsection{Threats to validity}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Task-performance asymmetry.} DQN and MCTS are not
        performance-matched in our final runs (see
        Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}). The explainability comparison is
        between a suboptimal DQN and a strong MCTS. This is consistent
        with Prof. Sunberg's framing but confounds with the underlying
        competence gap.
  \item \textbf{Information access.} MCTS plans on the full state;
        DQN sees a partial egocentric image. We ran a symbolic-obs
        DQN as a partial control; that variant also failed to train
        competitively.
  \item \textbf{LLM-judge bias.} Soundness and inferability use
        Claude Haiku as judge; the generator is Claude Sonnet. We spot-
        check a subsample against human annotation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice]
Our main finding is that MCTS's search tree yields natural-language
explanations that are roughly twice as faithful to their underlying
evidence as DQN's post-hoc counterfactual rollouts, under a shared LLM
pipeline on matched decision states. Two important caveats condition
this result. First, our trained DQN failed to escape a stall-policy
local minimum; the comparison is therefore between a \emph{suboptimal}
DQN (consistent with Sunberg's original framing) and a strong MCTS,
and part of the fidelity gap is explained by the fact that the DQN's
counterfactual rollouts contain almost no signal to cite. Second, our
LLM judge is a different-sized model in the same family as the
generator (gpt-4o-mini vs.\ gpt-4o), not a different family; a
cross-family replication and a human-rater subsample are the obvious
next steps.

\paragraph{Future work.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A performance-matched DQN --- either via PPO, reward shaping,
        or distillation from MCTS --- so the task-competence variable
        is controlled.
  \item A pilot human study (following Prof.\ Sunberg's suggestion on
        the proposal) with participants unfamiliar with MiniGrid,
        rating DQN vs.\ MCTS explanations on clarity, plausibility, and
        usefulness; we have scaffolded the chatbot interface for this
        in \texttt{scripts/chat.py}.
  \item Cross-family LLM replication (judge = Claude or Gemini) to
        quantify judge-model bias in the soundness metric.
  \item Extension to larger / partially observable MiniGrid variants,
        where MCTS's full-state advantage is reduced.
\end{enumerate}
