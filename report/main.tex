\begin{abstract}
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice]
We investigate whether online planning with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)
produces higher-quality explanations than post-hoc counterfactual analysis
of a deep Q-network (DQN). Both agents are trained or planned on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, a stochastic grid MDP, and
evaluated under a shared evidence-to-explanation pipeline: structured
per-action statistics are generated from each agent's decision process
(counterfactual rollouts for DQN, the search tree for MCTS), passed
through an LLM-based natural-language explainer, and scored on fidelity,
soundness, and post-hoc inferability. Our central question, following
Prof. Sunberg's framing: is the \emph{suboptimality} of an online
planner more explainable than the suboptimality of a learned policy,
specifically because the planner's ``training data'' is recorded in the
tree?
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% [DRAFT — write last, motivated by actual findings]
Reinforcement learning agents choose actions, but often the reason for a
choice lives inside opaque neural network weights. Online planners, by
contrast, construct an explicit deliberation record — the search tree —
before each action. We study whether that structural difference yields
measurably better natural-language explanations when both agents are
asked to justify the same decision.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A from-scratch MCTS planner for
        \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} with a tree-dump format
        designed for downstream explanation.
  \item A policy-agnostic counterfactual rollout framework producing
        DQN-side evidence in a format structurally identical to the
        MCTS tree statistics.
  \item Three automated metrics for LLM-generated explanations:
        fidelity, soundness, and post-hoc inferability.
  \item Empirical results comparing the two paradigms on matched
        decision states.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
% [DRAFT — ≥5 citations required, each with a short relation blurb]
\paragraph{Explainable RL.}
Milani et al.~\cite{milani2024xrl} survey explanation methods for
reinforcement learning, distinguishing \emph{transparent} models (where
the policy structure itself is interpretable) from \emph{post-hoc}
approaches (where behavior is explained after the fact). Our DQN
pipeline is post-hoc; our MCTS pipeline is closer to transparent since
the tree is the policy's deliberation.

\paragraph{Counterfactual explanations.}
Gajcin and Dusparic~\cite{gajcin2024cf} propose generating
counterfactual explanations by perturbing state features and measuring
downstream returns. Amitai et al.~\cite{amitai2024coviz} visualize
action-outcome counterfactuals in RL environments. We operationalize
this idea concretely as forced-first-action rollouts and compare to
tree-based evidence.

\paragraph{MCTS and interpretability.}
Baier and Kaisers~\cite{baier2021explain} discuss what is salient in a
search tree and how to surface it. We borrow their emphasis on
visit-count ratios and per-child value contrasts as the primary
evidence MCTS offers.

\paragraph{LLMs for explanation.}
Belouadah et al.~\cite{belouadah2025llm} (CITATION TO BE VERIFIED —
replace with Milani 2024 metrics section or Hoffman et al. 2018 if
unavailable) propose fidelity and plausibility metrics for
LLM-generated explanations of RL agents. Our metrics build on this
framework.

\section{Problem Formulation}
% Copy the authoritative formulation from docs/problem_formulation.md.
% Include S, A, T, R definitions verbatim.

We formulate \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} as a finite-horizon
MDP with factored stochastic transitions.

\paragraph{State} $s = (p_a, d_a, \{p^{(k)}_o\}_{k=1..4}, t)$, where
$p_a \in [1,6]^2$ is the agent's position, $d_a \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ its
direction (east/south/west/north), $p^{(k)}_o \in [1,6]^2$ each
obstacle's position, and $t$ the step counter.

\paragraph{Action} $A = \{0, 1, 2\}$ = \{turn left, turn right, move
forward\}.

\paragraph{Transition} $T(s' \mid s, a)$ factors into a deterministic
agent step followed by a stochastic per-obstacle random walk (each
obstacle attempts a uniform move to one of its 4 neighbors; blocked
moves leave the obstacle in place). An obstacle moving into the agent
(or vice versa) terminates the episode.

\paragraph{Reward} $R$ is $+ (1 - 0.9\,t/256)$ on reaching the goal,
$-1$ on collision, and $0$ otherwise. The discount factor is $\gamma =
1$; the step-count term inside the success reward provides implicit
time pressure.

\paragraph{Observability.} The MiniGrid default observation is a $7
\times 7 \times 3$ egocentric partial view plus a direction scalar.
The DQN is trained on this observation; MCTS plans on the full state
via a deep-copied simulator. We address this asymmetry in
\S\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{DQN Baseline (off-the-shelf)}
% [DRAFT — details in src/xrl/agents/dqn.py and configs/dqn_*.yaml]
We use Stable-Baselines3~\cite{sb3} DQN with a two-layer MLP
($256 \to 256$) policy. Observations are either flattened egocentric
image or the full symbolic state (14-dimensional). Hyperparameters are
in \texttt{configs/dqn\_*.yaml}.

\subsection{MCTS Planner (from scratch)}
Our MCTS implements textbook UCT~\cite{kocsis2006uct}: UCB1 selection
($c = \sqrt{2}$), full-width expansion at the root, rollouts under a
greedy-toward-goal heuristic policy (capped at depth 60), mean-value
backup, sampled-transition handling for stochastic obstacle dynamics.
Per-child counters record the rate of terminal success and collision
across every simulation passing through them — this is the tree-side
evidence.

\subsection{Counterfactual Framework}
For a decision state, ``counterfactual rollouts'' force each legal
action at step 0 then follow the trained DQN policy to termination. We
repeat $N = 100$ times per action and compute mean return, success
rate, collision rate, and mean steps-to-end with 95\% bootstrap
confidence intervals. The resulting \texttt{ActionStats} are
byte-identically structured whether the source is rollouts (DQN) or the
search tree (MCTS).

\subsection{LLM Explanation Pipeline}
A \texttt{DecisionRecord} (state + per-action stats + agent metadata)
is passed to Claude~\cite{anthropic} with a versioned system prompt.
The non-evidence portion of the system prompt is byte-identical between
the DQN and MCTS variants (enforced by a test); only the evidence
source header differs. Claude returns a JSON object with
\texttt{rationale}, \texttt{counterfactual}, \texttt{confidence}, and a
list of numerical \texttt{claims} suitable for automated verification.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Three per-explanation scores, aggregated per agent with bootstrap CIs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fidelity}: fraction of numerical claims in the
        explanation whose cited value matches the evidence within
        tolerance $\epsilon = 0.1$.
  \item \textbf{Soundness}: a separate LLM judge (Haiku 4.5) scores
        each rationale sentence on $\{0, 1, 2\}$ for support by the
        evidence. Reported as mean / 2.
  \item \textbf{Post-hoc inferability}: the judge sees the explanation
        with action numbers scrubbed and predicts the chosen action.
        Accuracy across decisions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Explanation quality}
\label{sec:expquality}
Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} and Table~\ref{tab:metrics} compare the three
metrics across evidence sources, using 45 matched decisions per agent.
% [DRAFT — rewrite in your voice; verify numbers.]
MCTS-tree-sourced explanations attain nearly double the fidelity of
DQN-rollout-sourced ones (0.94 vs 0.49) and substantially higher
soundness (0.90 vs 0.77). Post-hoc inferability is essentially tied
(0.96 vs 0.93; CIs overlap): both explanations carry enough signal for
a judge to recover the chosen action, but only MCTS explanations cite
numbers that actually match the evidence roughly twice as often.

We attribute the fidelity gap to the structure of each evidence source.
The MCTS search tree records per-action visit counts and empirical
success/collision rates from simulations \emph{that were actually
executed during deliberation}. In contrast, our DQN agent settled into
a degenerate stall policy (Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}), so counterfactual
rollouts from a decision state produce near-zero signal (nearly all
actions yield 0 return at the rollout-step cap). The LLM is asked to
justify a choice whose evidence row is essentially ``no outcome for any
action,'' and the explanation necessarily reaches for values that the
record does not actually contain.

\subsection{Threats to validity}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Task-performance asymmetry.} DQN and MCTS are not
        performance-matched in our final runs (see
        Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}). The explainability comparison is
        between a suboptimal DQN and a strong MCTS. This is consistent
        with Prof. Sunberg's framing but confounds with the underlying
        competence gap.
  \item \textbf{Information access.} MCTS plans on the full state;
        DQN sees a partial egocentric image. We ran a symbolic-obs
        DQN as a partial control; that variant also failed to train
        competitively.
  \item \textbf{LLM-judge bias.} Soundness and inferability use
        Claude Haiku as judge; the generator is Claude Sonnet. We spot-
        check a subsample against human annotation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% [DRAFT — summarise in 1 paragraph]
Our results are consistent with the hypothesis that MCTS's search tree
provides a more faithful substrate for explanation than post-hoc DQN
rollouts, but the task-performance gap between the two agents is a
significant confounder. Future work: a performance-matched DQN
(PPO-based or distillation from MCTS) and a human pilot study to
validate the automated metrics.
