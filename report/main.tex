\begin{abstract}
This paper evaluates the relative explainability of online planning and
deep reinforcement learning when both agents are asked to justify the
same decision. A deep Q-network (DQN) and a from-scratch Monte Carlo
Tree Search (MCTS) planner are operated on the
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} environment. For each agent,
per-action evidence is extracted at every decision state, counterfactual
Monte Carlo rollouts in the DQN case and the search tree in the MCTS
case, and passed through an identically-formatted large-language-model
explainer that produces a natural-language rationale, a counterfactual,
a confidence, and a list of verifiable numerical claims. The resulting
explanations are scored by an independent LLM judge on three metrics:
fidelity, soundness, and post-hoc inferability. Across 295 matched
decision states, MCTS-tree evidence produced explanations with fidelity
0.933 [95\% CI 0.92, 0.95] and soundness 0.891 [0.87, 0.91], as
compared to 0.437 [0.41, 0.47] and 0.758 [0.73, 0.78] for DQN-rollout
evidence. Post-hoc inferability was statistically tied at approximately
0.95 for both. The present work interprets this roughly two-fold
fidelity advantage as a direct consequence of the search tree recording
the deliberation that the explainer is subsequently asked to translate
into language, whereas a suboptimal DQN's counterfactual rollouts
contain little signal on which the explainer can draw.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
When a reinforcement learning agent chooses an action, the action
itself is observable, but the reasoning behind the choice is not. In a
deep Q-network, the action-value estimates that drive the decision
reside inside neural-network weights and are only indirectly
recoverable through post-hoc analysis such as counterfactual rollouts.
In contrast, an online planner such as Monte Carlo Tree Search commits
to an action only after constructing an explicit record of the futures
it considered, together with the outcomes it observed in each. The tree
is, in a very literal sense, the agent's deliberation.

This paper asks, following the framing of Prof.\ Sunberg, whether that
structural difference between a learned policy and an online planner
has a measurable effect on the quality of the natural-language
explanations each can support. Concretely, given the same decision
state and the same language model, do MCTS-sourced explanations cite
numbers that actually match the underlying evidence more often than
DQN-sourced explanations do, and are the rationales they produce more
sound?

To address this question, the present authors construct a shared
evidence-to-explanation pipeline and apply it to both agents on
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, a stochastic gridworld whose
adversarial obstacle dynamics are well-suited to separating competent
agents from incompetent ones. A structured decision record is extracted
at each real decision state, passed through an identically-formatted
LLM prompt that differs only in its evidence-source header, and scored
on three automated metrics.

The empirical finding, obtained over 295 matched decision states, is
that MCTS-tree evidence yields more than twice the fidelity of
DQN-rollout evidence (0.933 versus 0.437, with 95\% confidence
intervals that do not overlap) and substantially higher soundness
(0.891 versus 0.758), while post-hoc inferability is statistically
tied at approximately 0.95 for both sources. A plausible mechanism,
supported by the behaviour of the trained DQN, is that the
suboptimality of the underlying agent directly degrades the quality of
the evidence that is available for subsequent explanation. When a
DQN's counterfactual rollouts all yield approximately zero return, the
LLM is asked to justify a choice against an evidence row with little
signal in it.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The present work contributes: (1) a from-scratch MCTS planner for
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8} that emits per-decision
tree-statistics in a format suitable for downstream explanation; (2) a
policy-agnostic counterfactual-rollout framework that produces
DQN-side evidence in a structurally identical format to the MCTS tree;
(3) three automated metrics, fidelity, soundness, and post-hoc
inferability, implemented with bootstrap confidence intervals; and
(4) an empirical comparison of the two paradigms on 295 matched
decision states, using a real LLM generator and a held-out LLM judge.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Explainable reinforcement learning.}
Milani et al.~\cite{milani2024xrl} present a comprehensive survey of
explanation methods for reinforcement learning, drawing a distinction
between \emph{transparent} policies, such as decision trees and
symbolic rules, and \emph{post-hoc} methods that are applied to
otherwise opaque controllers. The DQN pipeline evaluated in the
present work falls into the latter category: counterfactual rollouts
are run after the policy has chosen an action, in order to reconstruct
the set of futures that action implied. The MCTS pipeline, by
contrast, is closer to transparent, since the tree is itself the
deliberation and its statistics are available for inspection without
any additional computation.

\paragraph{Counterfactual explanations for reinforcement learning.}
Gajcin and Dusparic~\cite{gajcin2024cf} propose counterfactual
explanations that perturb state features and observe the resulting
downstream returns, addressing the question ``what would the agent
have done if the state had looked different?''. Amitai et
al.~\cite{amitai2024coviz} focus on the complementary question ``what
would have happened if the agent had taken a different action?'' and
introduce the COViz visualisation. The evidence pipeline described in
Section~\ref{sec:approach} operationalises the latter form concretely,
as forced-first-action rollouts of fixed length, aggregated into
per-action means, success rates, collision rates, and bootstrap
confidence intervals. This choice was made so that the resulting
statistics are apples-to-apples comparable with the per-child
statistics that an MCTS search tree already produces during planning.

\paragraph{MCTS and interpretability.}
Baier and Kaisers~\cite{baier2021explain} argue that the MCTS tree is
a direct substrate for explanation precisely because it records what
the agent considered and what it found. They emphasise visit-count
ratios and per-child value contrasts as the most salient tree
quantities. The present work adopts these same quantities, together
with per-child empirical success and collision rates that are
instrumented during search, as the MCTS-side evidence.

\paragraph{Large language models for natural-language explanation.}
Recent work surveyed in~\cite{milani2024xrl} uses large language
models to translate structured evidence about a reinforcement-learning
agent into natural language. A well-understood failure mode of these
pipelines is hallucinated numerical claims, in which the model invents
statistics that are not present in the evidence record. The fidelity
metric defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics} is designed to quantify
exactly that failure mode. Belouadah et
al.~\cite{belouadah2025llm}\footnote{Citation to be verified before
submission; may be replaced with Hoffman et al.\ 2018 on XAI metrics.}
propose automated fidelity and plausibility metrics for
LLM-generated RL explanations. The three-metric suite in the present
work extends this framework with a post-hoc inferability check that
uses a held-out LLM judge.

\paragraph{Environment and planner.}
The benchmark environment used in the present study is
\texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}~\cite{minigrid}, a stochastic
gridworld whose adversarial obstacle dynamics are specifically chosen
to make sparse-reward model-free reinforcement learning difficult. The
MCTS planner follows the classic UCT formulation of Kocsis and
Szepesv\'ari~\cite{kocsis2006uct}, with per-simulation sampling of
the stochastic obstacle transition so that visit counts at the root
converge in expectation over the stochastic futures.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
The task is formulated as a finite-horizon Markov decision process
with factored stochastic transitions. In the notation of Kochenderfer
et al., the state, action, transition, and reward are as follows.

\paragraph{State.} A state is written as
$s = (p_a, d_a, \{p^{(k)}_o\}_{k=1}^{4}, t)$, where
$p_a \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes the agent's grid position,
$d_a \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ denotes its facing direction (east, south,
west, north), each $p^{(k)}_o \in \{1,\dots,6\}^2$ denotes one of four
obstacle positions, and $t \in \{0, \dots, 256\}$ is the step counter.

\paragraph{Action.} The action space is $A = \{0, 1, 2\}$,
corresponding to turn-left, turn-right, and move-forward.

\paragraph{Transition.} The transition $T(s' \mid s, a)$ factors into
a deterministic agent step, followed by a stochastic per-obstacle
random walk in which each obstacle attempts a uniform move into one
of its four orthogonal neighbours; moves blocked by walls, the goal,
or other obstacles leave the obstacle in place. An obstacle that
steps into the agent's cell, and equivalently an agent that steps
into an obstacle's cell, terminates the episode.

\paragraph{Reward.} The reward function is
\begin{equation}
R(s, a, s') =
\begin{cases}
1 - 0.9 \cdot t/256 & \text{if the agent reaches the goal} \\
-1 & \text{if a collision occurs} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The discount factor is $\gamma = 1$; the step-count term inside the
success reward provides implicit time pressure.

\paragraph{Observability.} The default MiniGrid observation is a
$7 \times 7 \times 3$ egocentric partial view, together with a scalar
direction indicator. The DQN agent is trained on this observation. The
MCTS planner, by contrast, plans on the full ground-truth state via a
deep-copied simulator. The present authors address this observability
asymmetry in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{DQN Baseline (Off-the-shelf)}
\textbf{Setup.} The DQN baseline leverages Stable-Baselines3~\cite{sb3},
configured with a two-layer MLP policy of width $256 \to 256$. Two
observation variants are considered: a flattened version of the
egocentric image, and a 14-dimensional symbolic encoding of the full
state. Hyperparameters for each variant are committed to
\texttt{configs/dqn\_*.yaml}. A distance-based potential-shaping
wrapper is also evaluated as a third variant, in an attempt to escape
a stay-still local minimum observed in the two raw-reward cases; the
resulting outcomes are reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{MCTS Planner (From-scratch)}
\textbf{Setup.} The MCTS planner implements textbook
UCT~\cite{kocsis2006uct} with UCB1 selection at exploration constant
$c = \sqrt{2}$, full-width expansion at the root (the action space is
small enough that no progressive widening is required), rollout under
a greedy-toward-goal heuristic capped at depth 60, and mean-value
backup. Because the obstacle transition is stochastic, each simulation
samples a fresh outcome from the environment's transition during
expansion and rollout; visit counts at the root therefore converge in
expectation to the post-decision outcome distribution. Per-child
counters track the rate of terminal success and terminal collision
across every simulation that passes through each child, and these
counters comprise the tree-side evidence that the explainer is asked
to translate.

\subsection{Counterfactual-rollout Framework}
\textbf{Setup.} At each decision state, the counterfactual-rollout
framework forces each legal action at step zero and then follows the
trained DQN policy to termination or a depth cap. This process is
repeated 20 times per action in the experiments reported below, and
the resulting returns, success flags, and collision flags are
aggregated into mean return, success rate, collision rate, and
bootstrap confidence intervals (1000 resamples, percentile method).
The resulting per-action records are structurally identical, field by
field, to the per-action records produced from the MCTS tree. This
structural parity is the property that allows the downstream LLM
pipeline to be agent-agnostic.

\subsection{LLM Explainer}
\textbf{Setup.} A \texttt{DecisionRecord}, comprising the state, the
chosen action, the per-action evidence, and a small agent-metadata
block (DQN Q-values or MCTS root-visit statistics), is passed to a
generator LLM using a versioned system prompt. The non-evidence
portion of the system prompt is byte-identical between the DQN and
MCTS variants; only the evidence-source header differs. This
constraint is enforced by a parity test on the prompt module, so
that the explainer pipeline is, as intended, a single-variable
contrast. The generator returns a JSON object consisting of a
\emph{rationale}, a \emph{counterfactual}, a \emph{confidence} score
on $[0, 1]$, and a list of numerical \emph{claims} suitable for
automated verification. Generation uses GPT-4o; the judge used in the
metric pipeline described below is GPT-4o-mini.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
Three per-explanation scores are reported, each aggregated per
evidence source with 95\% percentile-bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{\emph{Fidelity.}} For every claim in the explanation that
cites a per-action statistic, the metric compares the cited value
against the value present in the decision record. A claim is counted
as a hit if the values agree within tolerance $\epsilon = 0.1$. The
score is the fraction of claims that hit.

\textbf{\emph{Soundness.}} Each sentence in the rationale, together
with the counterfactual, is scored by an independent judge LLM on a
three-point scale: 2 indicates that the sentence is fully supported
by the evidence, 1 indicates partial support, and 0 indicates that
the sentence is contradicted by or invents information not present in
the evidence. The reported soundness score is the mean divided by two,
so that the result lies on $[0, 1]$.

\textbf{\emph{Post-hoc inferability.}} Every occurrence of the chosen
action's integer identifier is scrubbed from the explanation, and the
judge is asked to recover the chosen action from the scrubbed text
alone. The reported score is the judge's accuracy across decisions.

The judge is deliberately a different-sized model, GPT-4o-mini, from
the generator, GPT-4o, so that judge and generator do not coincide.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Task Performance}
Table~\ref{tab:taskperf} and Figure~\ref{fig:taskperf} report
success rate over 300 episodes per DQN variant, and over 50
episodes for MCTS. The random-action baseline succeeds 0.3\% of the
time, which confirms that the environment is non-trivial. All three
DQN variants settled into a policy that either moves rarely, resulting
in time-out at step 256, or occasionally collides; none exceeded 1\%
task-success rate. In contrast, the MCTS planner reaches the goal in
every one of 50 episodes, with mean return 0.934 and mean episode
length 18.3 steps. The performance gap is large and, given the
adversarial obstacle dynamics, consistent with prior observations that
sparse-reward model-free reinforcement learning is difficult on this
benchmark.

\subsection{Explanation Quality}
\label{sec:expquality}
Table~\ref{tab:metrics} and Figure~\ref{fig:metrics} report the three
explanation-quality metrics, evaluated on 150 DQN decisions and 145
MCTS decisions drawn from ten held-out trajectory seeds. MCTS-tree
evidence produced explanations with a fidelity of 0.933 and a
soundness of 0.891, as compared to 0.437 and 0.758 for the
DQN-rollout pipeline. The bootstrap confidence intervals on both
metrics do not overlap between agents, so the differences are
unambiguous at the sample size reported. Post-hoc inferability was
0.952 and 0.960 respectively, and the confidence intervals do overlap:
both explanation types carry enough signal for a judge to recover the
chosen action from the scrubbed rationale.

As such, the fidelity advantage of the tree-sourced explanations is
roughly two-fold, and the soundness advantage is approximately 0.13
in absolute terms. The present authors attribute this gap to the
structure of each evidence source. The MCTS search tree records
per-action visit counts and empirical success and collision rates
from simulations that were actually executed during deliberation.
Counterfactual rollouts from a DQN's decision state, in contrast, were
computed from a policy that settled into a near-stall behaviour. The
resulting evidence row contains a near-zero return for every action
and a near-zero success rate for every action, so the LLM explainer
is asked to justify a choice against what is essentially an empty
record. That many of the DQN explanations are still rated as sound
(0.758) reflects the generator's tendency to fall back on Q-values,
which are present in the agent-metadata block; however the numerical
claims the generator makes about outcome distributions cannot be
supported by a record whose outcome columns are uniformly zero, which
is precisely what the fidelity metric detects.

\subsection{Qualitative Example}
\label{sec:qualitative}

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\textbf{\emph{Task-performance asymmetry.}} The DQN and MCTS agents
are not performance-matched: the DQN is, as reported in
Table~\ref{tab:taskperf}, a suboptimal controller. This is consistent
with the original framing of the research question, in which the
comparison is explicitly between a suboptimal learned policy and a
suboptimal online planner, but it does confound the explanation-
quality comparison with the underlying competence gap. Part of the
fidelity advantage that the MCTS-tree evidence enjoys is therefore
attributable to the richness of its evidence row rather than to the
paradigm itself.

\textbf{\emph{Information access.}} The MCTS planner operates on the
full ground-truth state, whereas the DQN observes a partial egocentric
image. A symbolic-observation DQN variant was trained as a partial
control for this gap, but that variant also failed to exceed 1\%
task-success rate and so did not yield a usable matched-observation
comparison.

\textbf{\emph{Judge-model bias.}} The soundness and post-hoc
inferability metrics both use GPT-4o-mini as judge, whereas the
generator is GPT-4o. The judge is therefore a different-sized model
within the same family, and a cross-family replication, for example
with a Claude or Gemini judge, is listed as future work.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper has presented an evaluation of two evidence sources for
natural-language explanations of reinforcement-learning agents:
post-hoc counterfactual rollouts from a deep Q-network, and the search
tree of a Monte Carlo Tree Search planner. Across 295 matched decision
states drawn from \texttt{MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-8x8}, the
MCTS-tree pipeline produced explanations that were, by the fidelity
metric, approximately 2.1 times more faithful to the underlying
evidence than the DQN-rollout pipeline (0.933 versus 0.437), and
noticeably sounder (0.891 versus 0.758), while being statistically
tied on post-hoc inferability (approximately 0.95 for both).

Two caveats condition this result. Firstly, the trained DQN failed to
escape a stall-policy local minimum, which means the comparison is
effectively between a strong MCTS and a suboptimal DQN. This is
consistent with the original research question, but part of the
fidelity gap is likely explained by the fact that a degenerate policy
produces a degenerate evidence row. Secondly, the judge LLM is a
different-sized model in the same family as the generator, so a
cross-family replication is needed before the soundness numbers can be
taken as robust to judge-model bias.

Simply put, the results of the present work suggest that, when an
agent's deliberation is already recorded in a structured form, as it
is in a search tree, the quality of the natural-language explanations
that an LLM can subsequently produce is noticeably higher than when
the deliberation must be reconstructed post-hoc from rollouts. The
suboptimality of the underlying agent directly limits the signal
available in the evidence, and therefore the quality of any
explanation derived from that evidence.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
The present authors see three natural extensions. Firstly, a
performance-matched DQN, obtained either via a policy-gradient method
such as PPO, via reward shaping stronger than what was attempted in
this study, or via distillation from MCTS, would control for the
task-competence variable. Secondly, a pilot human study, following the
suggestion of Prof.\ Sunberg on the proposal, would validate the
automated metrics against human judgements of clarity, plausibility,
and helpfulness; a chatbot interface suitable for such a study has
already been scaffolded in \texttt{scripts/chat.py}. Thirdly, a
cross-family LLM replication, with a judge drawn from a different
model family than the generator, would quantify the contribution of
judge-model bias to the soundness numbers reported above.
